Restoration of a TEN-TEC Omni VI

The Ten-Tec Omni VI is an HF Transceiver for the 160 through 10 metre WARC bands. It was designed by US company Tennessee Technology Inc. in the 1990s and at the time had a list price of $2585. It is still considered by some enthusiasts to have unmatched receive performance, particularly in CW mode. The model for restoration was purchased for Â£200, described as almost complete and working, but requiring full re-alignment (which could hide a multitude of problems!).

From the operator's manual:

OMNI VI is the culmination of two decades of engineering in high performance HF transceivers. It features an advanced phase locked loop synthesizer combined with an oven controlled crystal oscillator for superior phase noise performance and stability. A large LED display is used for ease of viewing the operating frquency. Two smaller displays for a 24 hour clock and RIT/XIT offset. The OMNI VI also provides 100 duplex memories, scratch pad memory, band registers, dual VFOs, direct frequency entry and a built-in Iambic keyer.
The OMNI VI covers all bands 160 through 10 meters, using CW (with full or semi break-in), SSB (with built-in speech processor), RTTY (true FSK or AFSK) and FM. Optional crystal filters can be installed in both the 6.3 MHz and 9 MHz IFs. Also included is automatic notch filter provided by a DSP processor, a programmable CW transmit offset, and a CW digital lowpass filter. Remote control of many of the transceiver functions is possible via a serial interface.

Part 1. The Logic Board
As an embedded Software Engineer, this is of particular interest. It uses an 80C32 microprocessor operating at 20 MHz, with 32K EPROM and 8K battery backed RAM, and a 2105 DSP processor with a serial CODEC to provide the automatic notch filter. A 20 MHz oven controlled crystal oscillator provides the reference frequency for the transceiver. This was the first problem - due do age the crystal had "cooked out" and was off frequency and unstable. An exact replacement part is no longer available as it was made from unobtainium. A newer device would certainly work but would not fit in the space available on the logic board.

Using today's technology most of this board could be replaced with a single IC.
